# Research & Validation Plan

StudentOS is currently a concept prototype built on assumptions, not evidence. This document outlines how those assumptions will be tested before further development effort goes in.

## Why this exists

It's easy to keep adding modules to a concept without ever checking whether the core idea holds up. This plan exists to force that check before any more building happens.

## Core assumptions to validate

1. Students actually feel the "disconnected apps" pain described in the README — not just in theory, but as a recurring, noticed frustration.
2. A unified dashboard is a more compelling solution than simply using better individual apps (e.g. a good calendar app + a good notes app, unconnected).
3. The specific module set (Health, Sleep, Nutrition, Workouts, Finance, Study, Habits, Goals, Notes, Calendar) reflects what students actually want tracked together — not just what was easy or interesting to prototype.
4. The Daily Briefing format (a single generated summary each morning) is something people would actually read daily, rather than ignore like a notification.

## Research plan

**Method:** 5–8 short (15–20 minute) conversational interviews with other students, ideally a mix of degree types and years, not just CS students.

**Format:**
- Start with open questions about how they currently manage their time, health, notes, and money — before showing them StudentOS at all, to avoid leading the answer.
- Then walk through the live demo and ask what feels useful, what feels unnecessary, and what's missing.
- Ask directly: "Would you actually use this, or is it just interesting to look at?" — these are different answers and worth separating.

**What counts as a signal vs. noise:**
- A signal: someone unprompted describing a real moment where disconnected apps caused a real problem for them.
- Noise: polite enthusiasm about the UI without a concrete use case attached.

## What happens with the findings

- If the core "disconnected apps" pain isn't showing up unprompted across interviews, that's a sign the problem framing needs rethinking before touching the product further.
- If specific modules get consistently skipped or called "not something I'd use," they get cut rather than kept out of sunk-cost attachment.
- Findings get logged below, dated, in plain language — no over-interpretation of a small sample.

## Out of scope for now

Formal surveys, A/B testing, or quantitative analysis aren't appropriate yet — the sample size and stage of the project call for qualitative conversations first. Those methods would create a false sense of rigor before the basic problem framing is even confirmed.
